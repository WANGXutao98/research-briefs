## 1. New Research Questions (Emerged Today)

### Question 1: What Makes Robot Demonstration Data "World-Model-Ready"?

**The Question**: Three tools appeared this week for robot data quality — LeVizAudit (spatial pose diversity), robot-data-eval-toolkit (episode statistics + reward analysis), and PantheonDataPlatform (petabyte canonical schema normalization). All three target the same problem — poor data kills downstream models — but all three measure properties that matter for *behavioral cloning* (spatial coverage, episode diversity). The question no one has asked: do the same quality axes predict *world model pretraining* performance? A dataset with excellent spatial coverage but sparse contact events (grasps, pushes, slip-recovery) might produce excellent BC policies while producing world models that can't predict contact dynamics. **Which data properties predict downstream world model quality**, independent of behavioral cloning metrics?

**Why It Matters Now**: This question is timely for two independent reasons. First, NVIDIA's Physical AI Dataset (open, 500K trajectories, 4.8M+ downloads, with force/torque logs) provides exactly the substrate: large, open, multi-modal, with labels that go beyond vision. Second, the field is about to scale robot pretraining datasets dramatically — PantheonDataPlatform signals petabyte-scale curation is coming. If WM-readiness criteria are not established before large-scale curation happens, datasets will be curated for the wrong downstream target. This is an opportunity to define a standard before the standard is needed.

**Why It's Tractable**: The experiment is a segmentation study on existing open data. Take the NVIDIA Physical AI Dataset (already open, no collection needed). Segment into quartiles by (a) spatial coverage (LeVizAudit criterion) and (b) contact event frequency (count transitions where force/torque magnitude exceeds a threshold). Train a small world model (≤100M params, 2-3 hours per run on H100x1) on each quartile. Measure prediction accuracy on held-out contact sequences (force trajectory prediction MSE at t+500ms). Primary question: does contact frequency quartile predict WM accuracy better than spatial coverage quartile? This can be answered in **1 week** with H100x1.

**Evidence**: `BPerju/LeVizAudit` (GitHub, June 16 2026); `parfum666/robot-data-eval-toolkit` (GitHub, June 10 2026); `Rahul-Vinoth/PantheonDataPlatform` (GitHub, June 13 2026); NVIDIA Physical AI Dataset (Hugging Face, open, force/torque logs); sana-wm-pipeline PSNR/ATE decorrelation finding (June 13, 141 tests passing).

### Question 3: Is There a "VLA Transfer to High-Precision Domains" Paper Pattern Emerging?

**The Question**: `Ryan-YU-socialism/endoscopic-vla-qwengroot` (GitHub, June 16) applies QwenGR00T-style VLA to endoscopic surgical robotics — the first signal of VLA architectures moving into high-precision medical domains. The pattern: take an open generalist VLA, fine-tune on a domain-specific dataset, evaluate on domain-specific tasks. The research question is not "does this work?" (it probably does, at least partially) but rather: **what domain-specific properties of surgical robotics — sub-millimeter precision requirements, haptic feedback channels, stereo endoscopic cameras — stress VLA architectures in ways that tabletop manipulation does not?** If the domain surfaces a fundamental limitation, the finding is generalizable. If it surfaces a data gap, the finding motivates a dataset paper.

**Why It Matters Now**: The endoscopic VLA signal is early (★0, just created June 16), but the pattern of "generalist VLA → domain specialization" will produce a wave of papers in the next 6-12 months across medical robotics, industrial assembly, and agricultural manipulation. The opportunity is to be the paper that characterizes *which* VLA limitations are domain-specific and which are universal — rather than being the 15th paper that reports "we fine-tuned VLA X on domain Y."

**Why It's Tractable (with a caveat)**: Tractability here depends entirely on surgical dataset access. If David cannot acquire a labeled endoscopic manipulation dataset, this question is not actionable. **However**, the structural question generalizes: replace "endoscopic robotics" with "contact-rich peg insertion" (which David can simulate) and ask the same question: what properties of the task violate VLA assumptions? The answer from AHEAD (arXiv 2606.02486) is already partially known: dynamic scene prediction. The gap is characterizing *failure modes by task property* rather than by architecture.

**Evidence**: `Ryan-YU-socialism/endoscopic-vla-qwengroot` (GitHub, June 16 2026); AHEAD (arXiv 2606.02486 — 0/30 on physical tasks for all baselines, 29/30 for AHEAD); Hy-Embodied-0.5-VLA (Tencent Hunyuan, ★48, open, full robot stack); `psychopathdev/parley` (speech-perturbation VLA benchmark, June 14 — same "what domain property stresses VLA?" pattern).

## 3. Quick Validation Experiments (1-2 Week Scope)

**Idea 1 (WM-Readiness) → Contact Frequency Probe**:

Setup: Download NVIDIA Physical AI Dataset (open on HuggingFace). Select 1,000 trajectories. Label each trajectory with contact event frequency (number of time steps where ||F_xyz||₂ > 5N). Split into quartile Q1 (low contact, 0-10 events/episode) and Q4 (high contact, >50 events/episode) — 250 trajectories each. Train a minimal world model (2-layer ConvLSTM or small Transformer, ≤50M params) on each split for 2 hours (H100x1). Evaluate: given 5 frames, predict frame 6 + force vector. Compare MSE on a held-out 50-trajectory contact-rich test set.

Expected result: Q4 (contact-rich training) produces lower force-prediction MSE. If the gap is ≥2×, WM-readiness is contact-driven, not coverage-driven, and the paper hypothesis is confirmed. If the gap is <1.2×, contact frequency is not the primary predictor and you need to search other axes (transition sharpness, action diversity) before committing to the paper.

GPU time: ~6 H100 hours (3 hours per split × 2 splits). **Can run this week on H100x1 while the anchor experiment runs on H100x8.**
